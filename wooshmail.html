WooshMail

Overview

For the WooshMail project, I was tasked with three assignments:

1. Design two task flows for an email marketing campaign web app called WooshMail: one flow for an “admin” user and the other for a “basic” user.
2. Test the user flows, and
3. Mockup the task flows.

Problem to Solve

Currently, email marketing web apps can have three problems:

a. The process can be clunky and confusing.
b. There are a lot of competitors so WooshMail will need to stay competitive and have similar features.
c. What features are accessible to each user role can be confusing.

For this project, the two flows will walk through the same screens, but with two different user roles. The first role, admin, will have the highest level of access while the second role, basic, will only have access to a few features. WooshMail seeks to improve the experience for both of these roles with their audience of small business owners and entrepreneurs. Overall, the goal is to increase user retention and the number of campaigns sent.

Iteration Explanation

These sketches were a little difficult to produce because I was unfamiliar with email marketing websites so I was unfamiliar with how the site would appear. For example, I was unsure how to label the navigation section because I did not know what users would exactly be looking for. However, after checking several sites from the competitor’s list, I designed the sketches to have similar features but have a clean and streamlined look. My overall intention was to design with simplicity for a person new to email marketing companies.

User Flow Testing

Task

Researcher: "In this research study, you will be asked to use a prototype of an email marketing website using this laptop. Can you navigate through the website to send an email? I will briefly explain how to use the prototype. It is simple. You can click anywhere you want on the app. However, the prototype will guide you where to click by highlighting the element you are supposed to click in blue color. This will help you proceed to the next page. Finally, you will complete this task twice, one as an administrator user and the other as a basic user."

User Flow Analysis

About half of the participants were unfamiliar with email marketing campaigns before they started the research study, and the other half knew about it but never used them in the real world. I believe adding a short description of what email marketing campaigns are is warranted. This could be added somewhere in the template/gallery page. In the basic prototype, my directions could have been explained a little more. I was pursuing a streamlined approach for users who are new to email marketing, but sometimes less is not always ideal. Thus, I could fix this in the future prototypes. Other changes to the prototypes would follow from participants’ frustrations. That is, the prototypes will need to be more clear with allowing users to revisit the editing page from the add recipient page and to properly label the “send page” so there is no confusion who the user is sending the email to.

Summary and Learnings

Redesing Summary

At the start of the redesign I thought about what colors would fit well with Wooshmail. I selected to use cool colors instead of warm colors because I thought they give a friendlier invitation to new users and small business owners who have never used email marketing before. The primary colors I used were light blue, medium blue and darker blue with green and purple serving as analogous colors. Next, I made the design changes I learned from the user flow research in the previous unit. For example, in the basic user role prototype, I included a reminder for the user to go back to editing the template from the “add recipient” screen. In addition, I added a message in the “send email” screen to help the user know they are sending the email campaign to their recipients and are not sending the email to the admin. Lastly, I used color where I thought it would be not only visually appealing, but also appropriate for the user. In other words, I utilized plenty of white space in areas where I did not feel was going to enhance the usability or experience of the prototype. As can be seen in the “add recipient” screen in the admin and basic user role prototype, I experimented with the colors purple and blue to see which colors gave me a more positive feeling.

Learnings

I learned a couple things from this project. First, beginning with the overall color palette, I knew I had to design the web app to elicit some happy or positive feeling from new users, because I felt that would set the tone for the entire site. As for testing the prototypes, I gained more confidence in conducting user research and learned to document participants' behavioral and facial expressions. Overall, I enjoyed this project and learned more about business-to-business design.
