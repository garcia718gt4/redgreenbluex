Project

Under the Google UX Design program, I designed a brief user flow for a fictional restaurant called, Anderson, which specializes in modern cuisine and targets young professionals.

Problem

More restaurants are adapting to the pandemic and want to offer a way to help their customers feel safer by switching from paper menus to digital menus.

Goal

Determine if the user can select an item from the menu and place their order. The project was completed from March to April 2021.

Responsibilites

Designing an app from conception to delivery: conducting interviews, paper and digital wireframing, low and high-fidelity prototyping, conducting usability studies, accounting for accessibility, and iterating.

User Research Summary

I conducted interviews and created empathy maps to understand the users I'm designing for. A primary user group identified through research was adults who prefer the ease and control of ordering meals on their phone.

This user group confirmed initial assumptions about restaurant customers such as, users want to feel safe from contagion while dining out. In addition, the experience of using a food ordering app at a physical restaurant location is new to users because they typically use food ordering apps mainly for delivery during the pandemic.

Pain Points

Safety

Working professionals feel uncomfortable sharing paper menus.

Accessibility

Restaurant apps often use images and text too small to easily read.

Information Architecture

Menu apps can appear clunky and unorganized.

Problem Statement

Davit is a busy therapist who needs a safer way to view menus because he does not want to share paper menus with others during the pandemic.

User Journey Map

Davit’s journey revealed how helpful it would be for users to have access to a digital menu.

Usability Study: Parameters

Study Type

Moderated

Location

San Diego, CA

Participants

5

Length

10 - 15 minutes

Usability Study: Findings

Clarity

People want to easily see the "add" button.

Familiarity

Users want familiar icons and do not want to think too much about the meaning of an icon.

Assurance

People want to feel their order was successfully placed.

High Fidelity Prototype

Study Insights

1. One actionable insight based on the usability study was to present the add button in a clearer way. Thus, I included the word “add” at the bottom of the menu item.


2. A second actionable insight was to change the payment icon from a dinner plate to a traditional restaurant check to improve the meaning of the icon.


3. I added a “wait time” screen following the “place order” screen after participants reported if there was a way to know how long their order would take.

Accessibility Considerations

1

Making sure the text was eligible for users was a primary accessibility consideration.

2

Showing clear images of the menu items was necessary to enhance viewing.

3

Using bold text in key areas allowed for a friendly navigation flow.

Takeaways

Impact

“The app looks clean and simple. I think it is a good idea to view the menu on your phone.”

What I Learned

Conducting the usability test allowed me to see that others do not share my views of icon meaning. This resulted in design changes that improved the use of the app.

Next Steps

Conduct a second usability study to validate the design changes and explore adding new convenience features, such as a delivery option.
